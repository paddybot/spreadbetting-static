Cautious investors unconvinced as Facebook buys Oculus

Oculus VR is a technology company revolutionizing video gamers’ virtual experience. The firm’s 3D virtual gaming headset has been described by Facebook chief Mark Zuckerberg as "different than anything I've ever experienced in my life." Currently, Oculus has sold 75,000 headsets to game developing engineers, despite not yet specifying its public release date. Zuckerberg was quick to point out that Facebook does not intend to enter the hardware market; its software and services will remain its underlying business. The social media giant’s founder has identified advertisement and sales of virtual goods as Oculus’ key growth areas, as opposed to device sales. Zuckerberg went on to explain that the acquisition is Facebook’s nest step as it moves with technology; "Mobile is the platform of today, and now we're also getting ready for the platforms of tomorrow."

Facebook’s stock has taken a real beating over the past three days; it had fallen from $67.26 on Monday morning at the opening bell to just $63.85 by Tuesday afternoon, before recovering somewhat to close out at $64.93. However prices plunged even further on Wednesday, losing a massive 6.94% to close at $60.38. Investor scepticism surrounding the Oculus acquisition is primarily based on two fronts. Firstly, Oculus’ virtual headset is not the only product of its kind; Sony and Microsoft are both developing similar devices. Analysts suggest the $2 billion deal could represent Facebook paying over the odds for a technology that Sony developed in-house at a fraction of the cost. Interestingly, Sony stock has climbed from $17.65 at the close on Monday to an opening price of $18.36 by Wednesday morning, a jump of almost 4%.  This gain was partially relinquished Wednesday as 1.1% was given up. Secondly, as mentioned earlier, Facebook is venturing into unchartered territory and as such will face new market risks.
