Google stock split – how, what and why?

  • Class A shareholders now own two Google shares; one Class A share and one Class C share.
  • Class A shares will preserve their voting rights, trading under the new ticker GOOGL.
  • Class C shares will have no voting rights and will trade under the original ticker GOOG.
  • Class B shares remain unaffected.

In effect, this means that current stockholders do not lose out. However, the development is seen by many as a move by Google’s hierarchy to bolster its control over the company. In the past when Google made an acquisition (Nest Labs), any stock that was offered in the deal resulted in associated voting rights changing hands also. Likewise, stock incentives to staff were accompanied by voting rights. It is now expected that future acquisitions and staff compensation will take the form of Class C shares, thus avoiding further dilution of voting privileges.

Google finalized its long awaited 2-for-1 stock split today, 3rd April 201

The move was greeted brightly by investors in early morning trading as both classes surged almost 3.5%, before retreating to close the day with gains of approx. 0.5%. Interestingly, Class A shares (GOOGL) maintained a spread of approx $1 on Class C shares (GOOG) throughout the session. Investors may be amused that the S&P 500 now tracks 501 stock issues for the first time (despite being made up of only 500 companies).
